How to Write a Dating App Bio That Actually Gets Matches

Your bio is the difference between getting swiped on and getting ignored. Here's exactly how to write one that stands out.

Let's be honest: most dating app bios are trash.

"Just ask" — ask what, exactly? "Love to laugh" — as opposed to people who hate laughing? "Looking for my partner in crime" — we get it, you watched Bonnie and Clyde once.

Your dating app bio is prime real estate. It's often the deciding factor between a right swipe and a left swipe. And yet, most guys treat it like an afterthought — or worse, leave it blank entirely.

Here's the thing: a great bio doesn't just tell her who you are. It gives her something to work with. Something to message you about. Something that makes you memorable in a sea of gym selfies and fish pics.

Let's fix that.

Why Your Dating App Bio Matters More Than You Think

Research from dating apps shows that profiles with bios get significantly more matches than those without. But it's not just about having words on the page — it's about having the right words.

Your bio does three things:

  1. It shows personality. Photos show what you look like. Your bio shows who you are.
  2. It creates conversation starters. The best bios give her an easy way to open a conversation with you.
  3. It filters for compatibility. The right bio attracts people who actually get your vibe and weeds out those who don't.

Think of your bio as your wingman. It should do the work of starting the conversation before you even match.

The Anatomy of a Great Dating App Bio

Every killer bio has a few key ingredients. You don't need all of them, but you need at least two or three working together.

1. A Hook That Stands Out

The first line is everything. She's scrolling fast. You've got maybe two seconds to make her pause and actually read.

Skip the generic openers. Instead, lead with something unexpected:

Weak opener
"Hey, I'm Mike. I work in finance and love hiking."
Strong opener
"I once got banned from a petting zoo for being too aggressive with the goats. It was a misunderstanding."

See the difference? The second one makes you want to know more. It's weird in a good way. It's memorable.

2. Specifics Over Generics

This is where most guys go wrong. They list vague interests that could apply to literally anyone.

"I love music, food, and traveling." Cool, so does everyone else on the planet.

Instead, get specific:

  • Instead of: "I love music" → Try: "Currently convinced that 70s yacht rock is peak music. Fight me."
  • Instead of: "Foodie" → Try: "I've been to the same taco truck 47 times this year. I know what I like."
  • Instead of: "Love to travel" → Try: "Got lost in Tokyo for 6 hours with a dead phone. Best day of my trip."

Specifics are interesting. Generics are forgettable. Always choose the specific detail over the broad category.

3. Something She Can Respond To

This is the secret sauce. Your bio should practically write her first message for her.

Pro tip: Include an easy "in" — a question, a challenge, or a controversial opinion that invites a response.

Examples that work:

  • "Unpopular opinion: pineapple on pizza is elite. Swipe right to argue."
  • "Looking for someone to be my plus-one to a wedding where I know exactly three people."
  • "My barista knows my order by heart. Not sure if that's a flex or a cry for help."

Each of these gives her something to grab onto. An opinion to challenge. A story to ask about. A joke to play along with.

4. A Little Vulnerability (But Not Too Much)

Here's a counterintuitive one: showing a little self-awareness can be incredibly attractive.

Not insecurity — that's different. But admitting something slightly embarrassing or goofy shows you don't take yourself too seriously.

Good vulnerability
"I'm embarrassingly competitive at mini golf. Just warning you now."

This works because it's relatable, it's honest, and it hints at what a date with you might look like.

What to Avoid in Your Dating App Bio

Some things are instant left-swipes. Avoid these at all costs:

  • Negativity. "Don't swipe if you..." or "Not here for..." — you're already complaining and you haven't even matched yet.
  • Demands. "Must be under 5'8" or "Only message if you're serious" — this isn't a job posting.
  • Clichés. "Partner in crime," "looking for the Pam to my Jim," "fluent in sarcasm" — she's seen these 500 times today.
  • Self-deprecation. "Not sure why I'm on here lol" or "I'm probably out of your league anyway" — confidence is attractive, this isn't it.
  • Nothing at all. A blank bio says you either don't care or don't have enough personality to fill 200 characters.

Platform-Specific Bio Tips

Different apps call for slightly different approaches. Here's the breakdown:

Tinder Bio Tips

Tinder is fast-paced and casual. Keep it short, punchy, and funny. Two to three lines max. The vibe is playful, not serious.

Read more in our Tinder openers guide.

Hinge Bio Tips

Hinge gives you prompts to answer. Use them wisely — they're essentially structured bios. Be genuine, but still entertaining. This is where specifics really shine.

Our complete Hinge messaging guide covers this in depth.

Bumble Bio Tips

On Bumble, she messages first. Your bio should make that easy for her. Include clear hooks and conversation starters. Don't make her work to figure out what to say.

Bio Templates That Work

Need a starting point? Here are some structures you can adapt:

The "Two Truths and a Lie" Bio

Example
"Three facts about me: I've met a president, I can juggle, and I've never lost a game of Connect Four. One of these is a lie — and it's not the one you think."

The "This or That" Bio

Example
"Beach > mountains. Coffee > tea. Sleeping in > early mornings. If we disagree on more than one of these, this could get interesting."

The "Story Tease" Bio

Example
"Ask me about the time I accidentally ended up in a salsa competition in Havana. I didn't win, but I also didn't come last."

The "Self-Aware" Bio

Example
"Pro: Great at cooking. Con: My smoke detector can confirm. Looking for someone who appreciates ambition over results."

Test and Iterate

Here's the reality: there's no perfect bio that works for everyone. What works depends on your personality, your photos, and who you're trying to attract.

Try different approaches. If you're not getting matches, change it up. If you're getting matches but not quality conversations, your bio might be attracting the wrong people.

The best bio is one that:

  1. Sounds like you
  2. Attracts people you'd actually want to talk to
  3. Gives them something to message you about

Everything else is just experimentation.

The Bottom Line

Your dating app bio isn't just filler text. It's your chance to stand out in a crowded feed, show some personality, and make it stupidly easy for her to start a conversation with you.

Put some thought into it. Be specific. Be memorable. And most importantly — actually sound like someone worth talking to.

Because the truth is, your bio is often the difference between a match who ghosts and a match who actually shows up to the date.

Write better bios. Get better matches.
